Church Interior & Grounds

Icons, frescoes, and the lives of the saints depicted in our nave

The vaulted ceiling of the first half of our nave bears 24 icons of the saints, predominantly from the Old Testament. During tours of the Church, many visitors have asked about the icons and the saints depicted above them — their questions gave root to this record of each of the twenty-four icons, accompanied by a biography of the saint.

Upon entry into the Church, on the walls of the nave there are six icons of Russian Clerical Saints — three on the right and three on the left — each lit by a lampada.

We hope that these entries will be of interest to you, as well as educational and inspirational.

Vaulted Ceiling — First Half of Nave

The 24 Ceiling Icons & Lives of the Saints

The saints depicted are predominantly from the Old Testament but have definite influence on our Orthodox Faith today. Click any saint to read their full biography.

The Church also has a full interior photo slideshow of 41 images available.

Saint Eve

The first woman on earth, the first wife, and the first mother — known as the “Mother of All the Living.” Made in God’s image to be a helper to Adam.

Saint Adam

The first man on earth, made in the image of God. Adam was the first zoologist, landscaper, and horticulturist, given responsibility over creation.

Saint Melchizedek

“King of Righteousness” and “King of Salem.” Though not a Jew, he worshiped the one true God and blessed Abraham. Many scholars see him as a type of Christ.

Saint Noah

In a world taken over by evil, Noah was the only follower of God remaining. Blameless in his generation, he walked with God — and his life of 950 years exemplified obedience.

Saint Abraham

The founding father of the Jewish nation, a man of great faith and obedience. “He is the father of us all” (Romans 4:16). God visited Abraham on several unique occasions.

Prophet Isaiah

Lived in Jerusalem 2,700 years ago. His book contains many prophecies interpreted as being about the Messiah Jesus Christ, including the virgin birth (Isaiah 7:14).

Holy Martyr Longinus the Centurion

Roman soldier who stood watch at the Crucifixion. Witnessing the death of Christ and the great portents, he confessed: “Truly this was the Son of God.”

Prophet Zacharias & Saint Elizabeth

Zacharias was a priest of the Old Testament, struck mute by the Angel Gabriel for his disbelief, his voice restored at the birth of John. Elizabeth, his wife, greeted the Virgin Mary as “mother of my Lord.”

Saint Simeon

A just and devout man, promised by God he would not die before seeing the Messiah. When he received the infant Jesus in the Temple, he proclaimed: “Lord, now lettest thou Thy servant depart in peace.”

Saints Joachim & Anna

The holy and righteous parents of the Theotokos and grandparents of Jesus Christ. After fifty barren years of marriage, God granted them the Blessed Virgin Mary through the intercession of the Archangel Gabriel.

Saint John the Baptist (Forerunner)

The last of the Old Testament prophets, cousin of Christ, who baptized Jesus in the Jordan. Called the “Angel of the Desert” and the patron saint of godparents.

Saint Job

An extremely righteous and prosperous man whose faith was tested by Satan with the permission of God. Though stripped of everything, Job did not curse God — and was restored twofold.

King David

Perhaps the greatest ruler of Israel, a prophet, and ancestor of Christ. Author of many Psalms, David prefigures our Lord in numerous ways — the Lord’s Anointed, shepherd, and suffering servant.

Saint Mary of Magdalene

One of the first witnesses of the Resurrection of Jesus. Honored in Eastern Orthodoxy as a Myrrhbearer and “Equal to the Apostles.” She retired to Ephesus with the Theotokos.

Prophet Aaron

Brother of Moses and High Priest of Israel. Aaron stood in the gap between the people and their deliverer, serving as Moses’ mouthpiece and interceding before God on behalf of the people.

Prophet Elias (Elijah)

A man who saw God, a wonderworker and zealot for faith. He closed the heavens, called down fire, and was borne to heaven in a fiery chariot. Orthodox tradition teaches he was taken to heaven soul and body.

Ceiling Icons — Heavenly Hosts

The Holy Archangels

Also depicted among the ceiling icons are the holy Archangels — the Supreme Commanders of the Heavenly Hosts.

Archangel Jehudiel

One of the seven Archangels in Eastern Orthodox tradition, depicted holding a crown and a three-thonged whip. Patron of all who work to the glory of God; advisor and defender of those in positions of leadership.

Archangel Gabriel

Messenger of God, who announced the births of John the Baptist and Jesus Christ. Patron saint of messengers and those in broadcasting and telecommunications. Feast: November 8.

Archangel Michael

Archistrategos — Supreme Commander of the Heavenly Hosts. Leader of God’s armies against Satan. Patron of Kiev, the military, and police officers. Depicted as an angelic warrior with helmet, sword, and shield.

Archangel Raphael

“It is God who heals.” Raphael performs all manners of healing and is the patron of medical workers, matchmakers, and travelers. Depicted standing atop a large fish, a reference to the Book of Tobit.

Wall Icons — Nave Entrance

Six Icons of Russian Clerical Saints

Upon entry into the Church, on the walls of the nave there are six icons — three on the right and three on the left — lit by lampadas, depicting Russian Clerical Saints.

Saint Alexis, Metropolitan of Moscow

Metropolitan of Kiev and all Russia (1354), presided over the Moscow government during Dmitrii Donskoi’s minority. Canonized 1448. Patron saint of Moscow.

Saint Tikhon of Zadonsk

Bishop and theologian of the 18th century, known for his deep asceticism, compassion for the poor, and profound spiritual writings on the inner life.

Saint Dimitri of Rostov

Metropolitan of Rostov, renowned for compiling the Lives of the Saints (Chetyi Minei), one of the most important works of Russian Orthodox literature.

Saint Germogen, Patriarch of Moscow

Patriarch of Moscow during the Time of Troubles. Imprisoned by Polish invaders, he continued to exhort the Russian people to resistance, dying in captivity in 1612.

Saint John of Tobolsk

Metropolitan of Tobolsk and Siberia, a prolific preacher and author who served the Church faithfully across the vast Siberian territories.

Saint Mitrofan of Voronezh

First Bishop of Voronezh, a model of pastoral care and spiritual fatherhood. Canonized 1832 — one of the first saints glorified in modern Russian Church history.
