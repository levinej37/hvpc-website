Parish History

The Russian Community & Holy Virgin Protection Church in Nyack, NY

The story of Holy Virgin Protection Church is inseparable from the story of the Russian émigré community in Rockland County — a community born from hardship, shaped by faith, and built with the hands of its own people.

The Beginning — 1941

The Tolstoy Foundation & Reed Farm

Miss Alexandra Tolstoy (youngest daughter of the author Leo Tolstoy) and Mrs. Tatiana Schaufuss organized the New York-based Tolstoy Foundation, whose mission was to help the many emigrants of Russian descent entering the United States. The foundation drew together many prominent Russian people such as Serge Rachmaninoff, Igor Sikorsky, Boris Sergievsky and many others.

In 1941, a generous donor made it possible for the Foundation to acquire a 70-acre farm (Reed Farm) in Valley Cottage, New York for the sum of $1.00. With this purchase, the history of the Russian Community in Rockland County began.

Tolstoy Farm provided a center where Russian refugees stayed until they could permanently resettle. It provided a home for orphaned children, a large operating farm supplying food and labor, and a summer camp for disadvantaged children. More than 6,000 refugees passed through Tolstoy Farm. A chapel was established from the first day; it was not until 1957 that a permanent gold-domed church in typical Russian style was built on site.

1949 – 1950

Russians Settle in Nyack

Nyack, with its beautiful riverfront location, plentiful factory jobs, and close proximity to Tolstoy Farm, was an attractive destination. The first few Russian families purchased homes in Nyack in 1949–1950, and others soon followed. Many found housing in the old “Elephant House” of the Clarkstown Country Club — today the location of Nyack Junior High School and Nyack College.

In their wanderings, Russian refugees established emotional roots by immediately setting up chapels, which formed the heart of their communities.

This was the site of the first Russian Orthodox chapel in Nyack. As the community grew, a large house on Midland Avenue (present site of the Nyack Field Club) was rented as the chapel became too small.

1951 – 1953

Searching for a Permanent Home

In 1951, Father Adrian Rimarenko purchased a Catholic convent on Smith Road in Spring Valley, New York, establishing a Russian convent there — today also the site of one of the largest Russian cemeteries in the United States.

The Nyack parish went through a series of rented locations: a garage on Depew Avenue behind the Wright Brothers building, then the hall of the Reformed Church on Broadway, then a third-floor location in a Broadway building (current location of the New Age Center).

In 1953, Father Seraphim Slobodskoy and his family arrived from Germany to take up energetic leadership of the parish. Plans began to build a permanent church.

1954 – 1957

Built by Parishioners

In early 1954, the parish purchased the property on the corner of Cedar Hill and Prospect Avenues. Fund-raising concerts (entry price was $1), lotteries, and brick purchases for 50 cents were held to raise money. Children, parents and grandparents worked together: digging, building, carving, and painting.

At last in 1957, the parish moved into the new Holy Virgin Protection Church — built entirely by parishioners.

1957 – Present

Growth & Community

Since that day, the parish has continued to grow, to flourish, and to settle Rockland County. The parish organized a Saturday morning school from the very beginning to teach religion, Russian language, history, geography, literature and culture. Over the years as many as 125 children have passed through the school each year. Some graduates have made careers in the State Department, international banks, and abroad — one graduate can be seen on television interpreting for Presidents and Secretaries of State.

Father Seraphim died on November 5, 1971 and was succeeded by Father George Larin. In February 1970, the very first Blini Festival was held at the West Nyack Fire Station to raise funds for a new school building and hall.

The new school building and hall was dedicated on October 14, 1980 — the parish feast day — in memory of Father Seraphim Slobodskoy, coinciding with the 25th anniversary of the consecration of the church building.

In 2002, the parish celebrated the 50th anniversary of the Russian school and honored Matushka Elena Slobodskoy, widow of Father Seraphim, for her 50 years of service to the school and the parish. Matushka Elena passed away October 1, 2003.
